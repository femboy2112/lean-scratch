## 3. Access to the Intrinsic Layer

The cogito establishes the I but gives only bare cognitive capacity and formal self-identity. The I's ability to verify, to reason, to recognize intrinsic structures, is not given by the cogito; it is acquired through interaction with a system that itself instantiates intrinsic structure.

### 3.1 The two senses of intrinsic-access

*Ontologically*, mathematics, logic, and reason are intrinsic — they hold whether or not any cogniting being is present to verify them, woven into the system the I is embedded in. *Epistemically*, a cogniting being's access to intrinsic structure is not given; it is acquired developmentally. Both senses hold simultaneously. The architecture is naturalist-realist about intrinsic structure (it exists in the system independently of any I) and developmental about intrinsic-access (the I builds its toolset over time).

### 3.2 Verification-tool imprinting

To the previously identified content imprinting (ρ-changes) and contact imprinting (κ-changes) is added:

**Verification-tool imprinting.** Through interaction with the system, the I acquires recognition capacities for intrinsic structures — verification tools that allow the I to identify conditional pathways, place contents in the φ-layered structure of Tier 3, and run reason-tests. Each tool corresponds to an intrinsic structure (object persistence to reflexivity, cause-and-effect to implication, modal recognition to alethic structure, induction to universal generalization).

Verification-tool imprinting has both focus-driven and osmotic components. The focus-driven side is governed by the same general dynamics as content imprinting: focus, contact, repeated encounter. What an I encounters repeatedly, with sufficient focus or contact-driven attention, becomes part of the I's explicit verification toolset. The osmotic side (Section 8.7, File 3) operates at substrate level: ambient exposure to instantiated intrinsic structures populates the substrate with proto-patterns — substrate-level features that are not yet explicit tools but that ground later explicit tool development. The arc from substrate-held proto-pattern to explicit deployable tool is gradual: osmotic imprinting prepares the substrate; Mode B operation, external prompting, or shadow encounter later brings the substrate-held pattern into explicit accessibility; explicit refinement produces a verification tool the I can deploy in φ-pathway construction. The osmotic substrate patterns continue operating throughout, alongside the explicit tools.

The "I had a sense of X before I could put it into words" experience that often precedes explicit articulation of verification tools is the architectural signature of this arc: the substrate held the pattern osmotically; explicit access came later.

### 3.3 Three kinds of in re encounter

**Direct world-encounter.** Some intrinsic structures are instantiated in the world at temporal scales the I can perceive directly. Object persistence, cause-and-effect, spatial relations, simple arithmetic of small collections, immediate logical constraints — these are encountered directly through ordinary sensorimotor engagement.

**Shadow encounter.** Some intrinsic structures are not instantiated in directly perceivable forms but leave consequences — *shadows* — in the structure of what is encounterable. The I, deploying its accumulated verification toolset on present content, recognizes structure that no single tool was sufficient to predict. Three operationally-recognizable forms:

(a) *Known pattern, unexpected location.* The I has a verification tool for some pattern. The pattern shows up in a context where the I didn't expect it to apply. The unexpectedness signals that something underlying is producing the appearance — the pattern's presence in this new context isn't accidental but is a trace of structure connecting this context to where the pattern was originally learned. *Exemplar:* Newton's recognition that the apple's gravitational fall (terrestrial domain, well-known pattern) and the moon's orbital motion (celestial domain, classically treated as structurally separate) were instances of the same underlying inverse-square attraction. The pattern's appearance in the celestial domain was the shadow trace of universal gravitation connecting the domains.

(b) *Composite of known patterns, not recognized as composite.* The I sees a result that doesn't match any single tool, but the apparent unfamiliarity dissolves when the I steps back and recognizes that what it's looking at is several known patterns interacting. The recognition wasn't of a new structure; it was of a higher-level organization of structures the I already had tools for. The trigger may be content encounter (the I sees something puzzling and recognizes it as a composite), but it may equally be *combinatorial action* on existing tools — the I deliberately or experimentally composes its own apparatus and then recognizes what the composition produces. *Exemplar:* a learner with separate tools for quadratic polynomials, the parabola height formula, and differentiation takes the derivative of a quadratic, sets it to zero, solves for x, and substitutes back, recovering the parabolic vertex. The recognition is not of a new mathematical object but of the structural interdependence between calculus, algebra, and geometric form — the tools were aspects of one deeper organization. The shadow event is the recognition of the unity the composite reveals.

(c) *Apparent pattern subcomposed of multiple sub-patterns.* The I thought it was looking at one pattern, but on closer attention the apparent unity decomposes into multiple distinct patterns operating in concert. The original recognition was coarse-grained; finer-grained attention reveals that what looked like one structure was several. This applies at any scale: the apparent unity may be small (a particle), human-scale (a behavior), or large (a matter distribution); the operational signature is scale-independent. *Exemplar:* the historical decomposition of matter — atoms originally treated as fundamental units, then decomposed into protons, neutrons, and electrons under finer attention, then further decomposed into quark interactions (with electrons remaining fundamental at that level). Each step is a Form 3 shadow event: the apparent single-scale unity revealed as multi-scale composite under attention-shift.

In each form, the I's existing tools are what enable the recognition — the I cannot detect a shadow without having tools whose adequacy or coverage the shadow makes visible. The recognition is therefore cave-internal: it does not require access outside the I's own reasoning, and it does not claim such access. What it does claim is that the recognized structure is a manifestation of intrinsic mathematical/logical structure realized in the system the I is embedded in, accessible through the cognitive event but not directly as such. The naturalist-realist commitment in Section 2.1 (File 1) is what licenses the move from "the I had a recognition event" to "the I encountered structure": the system contains pervasive mathematical correlations; the I, embedded in the system, encounters these correlations through cognitive events of the kinds described.

Shadow encounter is structurally connected to osmotic imprinting (Section 8.7, File 3). The "known pattern" in Form 1 is often osmotically held in substrate before its explicit recognition; the unexpected location triggers the substrate's pattern to surface into explicit access. The composite of Form 2 is often substrate-held in component form before the composite is explicitly recognized. The sub-decomposition of Form 3 is often substrate-held with sub-patterns that finer attention surfaces into explicit access. Shadow encounter is in part the I gaining explicit access to substrate patterns it has been osmotically holding — the "Aha" of recognition reflects substrate patterns becoming explicitly available rather than the formation of entirely new patterns from scratch.

**Self-encounter through meta-reasoning.** Once the I has accumulated a verification toolset, those tools and the I's own structure become themselves objects available to the I's attention. Reason on reason. Meta-reasoning is constrained by the developmental escalator: the I can only attend to structure it has accumulated; meta-reasoning does not bootstrap from nothing. Once enough lived-I structure exists, meta-reasoning becomes possible and yields the most abstract verification tools.

Self-encounter through meta-reasoning has two distinguishable sub-types:

*Pure self-encounter.* The I attends to its own contents, reasoning, integrations, or position directly, without external in re instantiation playing a triggering role. The cogito itself is the simplest instance: the I taking itself as object, recognizing its own self-identity. Deliberate introspection, recognition of one's own thought-patterns, attention to one's own cognitive activity — these are pure self-encounter. The I's own cognitive activity instantiates intrinsic structure (the I is part of the system; its reasoning instantiates structures the system contains), and the I can encounter it through attention to itself.

*Externally-mediated self-encounter.* External in re instantiation reveals the I's own structural position to itself. Standing on a mountaintop and seeing the trees below as ants, then recognizing one's own scale-position relative to even greater systems, is paradigmatic: the not-I scene is what makes self-position cognitively accessible; without the external instantiation, the recognition would not occur. Other examples include encountering a description of a pattern one matches and recognizing the match, looking at one's reflection and recognizing the reflective relationship rather than just the body, encountering a system whose structure puts one's own position in relief. In externally-mediated self-encounter, the load-bearing event is self-encounter (the content is one's own structural position), but external in re instantiation is essential to the encounter occurring.

Externally-mediated self-encounter typically involves multiple pathway-orders simultaneously: first-order perception of the external scene, second-order cognitive recognition of self-position via the perspectival nesting the scene reveals, and often third-order affective response (the humbling that accompanies recognition of one's smallness in a vast system; the awe that accompanies seeing the scale of what one is part of). The architecture's pathway-typology (Section 8.5, File 3) handles this multi-order character without requiring new commitments.

More elaborate meta-reasoning yields formal logic about formal systems, mathematics about mathematical structures, and the apparatus of philosophy. These can occur as pure self-encounter (working out a proof in pure introspection) or as externally-mediated (a diagram in a textbook revealing structure the I had not previously recognized). Both are meta-reasoning; both produce the most abstract verification tools.

### 3.4 The temporal-scale escalator

The order in which verification tools are imprinted is structured by the temporal scale required to perceive each tool's *in re* form.

Object persistence is encounterable at scales of seconds. Cause-and-effect at slightly longer scales. Modal structure at longer scales still — observing a system long enough to notice that what happened is one trajectory among several the system's structure supports. Hypothetical reasoning at scales where the I observes systems under varying conditions. Mathematical induction at scales sufficient to observe pattern-repetition across many instances.

This generates a developmental escalator. Shorter-timescale tools imprint first because the I encounters their instantiations more frequently. Each stage's tools enable the I to integrate contents more stably across time, which allows comparison across longer intervals, which enables perception of longer-timescale in re structures. The order is environment-shaped within universal form.

### 3.5 Variation between I's

Verification capacity varies between I's. Variation arises from substrate (different focus capacities, different neural structures), environment (what is instantiated for the I to encounter, at what salience, with what frequency), accumulated lived-I structure (what tools are available at each stage to enable next-stage acquisition), and the focus dynamics that determine which structures actually receive enough engagement to imprint.

The architecture predicts variation rather than presupposing uniformity. Different I's, given different substrates and different environments, will end up with different verification toolsets. The same content may be φ-placeable for one I (verification pathway constructible from that I's toolset) and not for another (no verification pathway constructible).

### 3.6 Closing the verification-pathway circularity

The criterion for "apparent verification pathway" was previously informal. It now grounds out. An apparent verification pathway is one that uses verification tools the I has acquired through prior imprinting. Tools are acquired through encounter with *in re* forms of intrinsic structure (direct, shadow, or self-encounter). Encounter is grounded in the system's pervasive instantiation of intrinsic structure (Section 2.1, File 1). No remaining circularity.

## 4. Modes of I-Development

The cogito as formal anchor is in place from $t = 0_{T_m}$ for any cogniting being. The cogito as developmental act — the I's actual capacity to perform reflexive self-recognition and the actualization of the I as a differentiated agent with lived structure — is acquired developmentally. This section identifies three distinct modes by which the lived I emerges.

### 4.1 Modes are response-types, not predictive mechanisms

**Important framing**: the three modes (A, C, B) are *response-types* — categories of developmental response that an I can have to encounters with not-I or with itself. They are not predictive mechanisms. The architecture does not predict, for a given encounter at a given time for a given I, which mode will fire. Mode-selection depends on substrate, history, environment, and stochastic elements that the architecture does not formalize.

What the architecture predicts is that *if* a particular mode operates, certain structural consequences follow: Mode A produces boundary; Mode C produces integration; Mode B produces self-attention and meta-reasoning capacity. The selection rule between modes for any specific encounter is application-level work, not foundation-level commitment.

This is consistent with how good structural taxonomies operate: thermodynamics doesn't predict whether a specific gas molecule will move left or right at a specific moment, but it predicts what kinds of motion are possible and what statistics hold over many such motions. The architecture's modes are similar — they classify response-types and their consequences, without committing to a per-encounter selection function.

The phrase "sufficiently distanced" in Mode A is therefore a *placeholder* for the unformalized factors that produce mode-selection (substrate state, history, contact dynamics, environmental specifics, stochastic elements). The architecture acknowledges this is a placeholder, not a defined predicate, and treats the specification of the selection rule as future work that may or may not be fully formalizable.

### 4.2 Mode A: Adversarial differentiation

When the modeling I encounters an item in primal not-I that registers as discrepant from what the I is currently integrating — out of place from the I's frame of reference — three components combine to produce I-emergence through this mode.

**The otherness-trigger.** The encounter generates an automatic registration of discrepancy. The not-I appears foreign, wrong-from-here, out of place. This is not chosen; it is a structural response when the relevant conditions (which the architecture does not fully specify) obtain.

**The prerogative of consistency (frame-privileging).** The discrepancy is not registered neutrally. The I's frame functions as the implicit consistency-standard against which the not-I is measured. The not-I that conflicts with the I's frame is registered as inconsistent — its integration would damage the I's structural consistency. The I's frame is treated, implicitly and structurally, as the consistency-anchor. This is not about correctness in any moral or epistemic sense; it is about the I's structural pull toward preserving its own internal consistency. The I does not deliberate "perhaps my frame should change"; the I's emergence is itself the structural privileging of its own frame as the consistency-standard against which discrepancy registers.

This component is the *prerogative of consistency* (PtCns): the structural feature by which the I's frame is the implicit consistency-standard. It is fundamental to lived consciousness — without it, encounters with not-I would not register against any standard; Mode A and Mode C would lack the criterion that distinguishes consistency-damaging from consistency-extending not-I; the I/not-I boundary would not form. PtCns operates in all Mode A encounters: with discrepant objects, contradicting facts, violated expectations, misbehaving systems, and (as a special case) other I's whose frames conflict with one's own. Section 9.5 (File 4) develops the inter-I case specifically; this component covers the general phenomenon. The unification of Modes A and C under PtCns is developed in Section 4.5 below.

**The organic creation of the I through adversarial relation.** The lived I emerges as a differentiated agent through this boundary-drawing pressure. Without encounter with sufficiently-distanced not-I, the formal cogito anchor would still be in place, but the developmental capacity to act as a differentiated agent would not actualize. The I is constituted by its differentiation from not-I.

Mode A produces *the I/not-I boundary*. It is the mechanism by which the I emerges as a separable agent in the first place. Subsequent ρ-building presupposes that some boundary exists.

### 4.3 Mode C: Positive identification

When the modeling I encounters an item in primal not-I that does not register as discrepant — that registers as continuous with what the I is, or close to it, or comforting — the encounter produces a different response: *positive identification*. The not-I item is integrated as part of the (proto-)self rather than registered as discrepant.

This mechanism dominates in early development, before strong I/not-I differentiation has occurred. Things in the immediate environment — body parts, caregivers, comfort objects, food — get treated as continuous with self because there is not yet a clear boundary to exclude them.

The candy example is diagnostic. When candy is taken from a baby, the baby cries intensely because the candy has been positively identified as part of the (proto-)self. Loss of the candy is experienced as loss of self, not merely as denial of access to a desired object. The adult, in whom Mode A has done extensive work and the I/not-I boundary is mature, has differentiated the candy as not-self. Loss is felt as loss-of-thing, not loss-of-self.

Mode C *populates inside the boundary*. Most of the lived-I network is built through Mode C. Adversarial differentiation establishes the boundary; positive identification fills the inside.

### 4.4 Mode B: Reflexive meta-reasoning

Mode B is the I's reflexive-attention capacity: the I turns toward itself, the I's structure becomes object of attention, the cogito-as-developmental-act actualizes. Mode B requires *some accumulated structure* to be available as object of attention. The I cannot perform meta-reasoning at $t = 0_{T_m}$ because there is nothing yet accumulated to reason about. But once Mode A has established a boundary and Mode C has populated some lived-I structure, the I has material to which reflexive attention can turn.

Mode B has two distinguishable sub-types based on what triggers the reflexive move:

*Internally-triggered Mode B.* The I *just happens upon* a reflexive move without external trigger. The I turns toward itself spontaneously; the cogito-as-developmental-act actualizes through internal stochastic dynamics. Pure introspective shifts, sudden self-recognition during quiet reflection, the "wait, what am I doing?" moment that arises without external prompt — these are internally-triggered.

*Externally-triggered Mode B.* External in re instantiation triggers the reflexive move. Standing on a mountaintop and recognizing one's scale-position relative to vast systems; hearing a question that forces self-reflection ("why do you do that?"); encountering a piece of art that triggers recognition of something about oneself; having someone else's behavior make one's own pattern visible by contrast; reading a passage that names something one had been doing without naming — these are externally-triggered. The triggering external in re instantiation is essential; without it, the reflexive move would not have occurred at this moment. But the operation itself is Mode B (the I taking its own structure as object), not direct or shadow encounter (the content is the I's own position, not external structure).

Externally-triggered Mode B and externally-mediated self-encounter (Section 3.3) are the same operation viewed from the I-development side and the verification-tool-imprinting side respectively. The mountain experience is simultaneously externally-triggered Mode B (refining the I's structure through self-attention) and externally-mediated self-encounter (imprinting meta-reasoning verification tools relevant to self-position).

**Mode B firing probability is stochastic with environment-shaped odds.** The probability distribution over Mode B firings is shaped by environment in two ways. First, settings rich in stimuli that *trigger* externally-mediated Mode B (mountains, art, philosophical conversation, structured questions, perspective-revealing situations) increase the per-encounter probability of externally-triggered firings. Second, settings rich in stimuli that *prompt* internal reflection (puzzles requiring self-monitoring, contemplative practices, philosophical or mathematical content, conversation that invites introspection) increase the per-encounter probability of internally-triggered firings. Settings poor in either kind of stimulus decrease the corresponding probability. Within an environment, whether any given encounter actually produces a Mode B firing is stochastic.

**Onset distribution.** Across cogniting beings, the developmental onset of Mode B (the age at which meta-reasoning capacity emerges and stabilizes) is distributed across a population. The architecture's structural commitments require:

- *Zero at developmental origin.* The distribution must cross zero at $t = 0_{T_m}$. No I has Mode B capacity at the moment of cogito-anchoring, because Mode B requires accumulated structure to attend to and no structure has yet accumulated.
- *Non-zero later.* The distribution must reach non-zero values at later developmental times for any cogniting being whose substrate and environment support Mode B onset at all.
- *Variation across I's.* Different I's, with different substrates and environments, reach Mode B capacity at different times. The architecture predicts variation, not uniformity.

Beyond these structural constraints, the architecture does not currently derive a specific distributional shape. A bell-shaped (approximately Gaussian) distribution is a natural intuitive guess — most I's gain self-cognition around a typical age with outliers earlier and later — but the architecture does not establish that the distribution must be bell-shaped rather than log-normal, gamma, Weibull, sigmoid-CDF, or some mixture distribution reflecting subpopulations with distinct trajectories. The choice of distributional shape is currently freestanding within the architecture: it fits casual observation but is not forced by foundational commitments.

The architecture's commitment is therefore the structural claim (zero at origin, non-zero later, variation across I's). The distributional shape is an empirical question about cogniting-being populations, addressable through application work that engages developmental psychology and individual-differences literature. Both internally-triggered and externally-triggered Mode B require accumulated structure; both follow the same structural-onset constraints; the specific distributional shape applies to both.

The structural-onset claim, even without specifying the distribution, gives the architecture a population-level prediction without making it normative: variation across I's is statistical regularity (with whatever shape the empirical distribution turns out to have), not prescription.

Mode B *refines the structure* through self-attention. It enables the I to take its own reasoning, integrations, and verification tools as objects of further reasoning. This produces meta-reasoning verification tools and enables sophisticated revision of lived-I integrations. Both sub-types contribute to refinement; the trigger is what differs, not the operation's structural consequences.

### 4.5 The three modes and the three kinds of encounter

The three modes of I-development (A, B, C) and the three kinds of *in re* encounter (direct, shadow, self-encounter through meta-reasoning, from Section 3) are related but distinct categorizations. The three kinds of encounter answer the question *how does the I gain access to intrinsic structure?* The three modes answer the question *how does the lived I form and its boundary establish?*

The connections:

- **Mode B and self-encounter through meta-reasoning** are the same operation viewed from different angles. The I attending to itself is both a mode of I-development (self-attention refining structure) and a kind of encounter that imprints verification tools (meta-reasoning tools). Both sub-type pairings correspond: internally-triggered Mode B ↔ pure self-encounter; externally-triggered Mode B ↔ externally-mediated self-encounter.
- **Direct and shadow encounters** are encounter-types; they can produce Mode A operation (when the encounter is sufficiently distanced) or Mode C operation (when integration occurs). The same encounter type can produce different developmental modes depending on the I's response.
- **Mode A and Mode C** are response-types to encounters; they describe what happens to the lived-I structure as a result of an encounter, not what kind of encounter occurs.

So encounters are inputs; modes are responses. The selection rule between Mode A and Mode C for a given encounter is not predicted by the architecture.

Both modes are governed by a single structural prerogative: the *prerogative of consistency* (PtCns; Section 4.2, generalized in Section 9.5, File 4). Mode A is typically PtCns-driven: discrepancy against the I's frame elicits differentiation when integration would damage frame-consistency. Mode C is PtCns-constrained: positive identification may be driven by affinity, attraction, admiration, recognition, or aspiration, but integration proceeds only insofar as the lived-I profile can maintain or restore coherence. PtCns is therefore not limited to contradiction-repair; it is the prerogative by which the operating lived-I preserves coherent profile-organization under encounter, including integration, rejection, differentiation, reweighting, and *coherent non-integration* (identity-neutral encapsulation, below). The architecture predicts the unifying principle (consistency-preservation) without specifying the per-encounter selection rule; what determines whether integration, differentiation, or encapsulation occurs in a specific encounter still depends on substrate, history, environment, and the stochastic factors Section 4.1 acknowledges as unformalized.

**Identity-neutral encapsulation.** No encounter, contact, or access is profile-neutral in the strict sense. Any content encountered by the I produces some development of the identity-correlation profile $P_{m,t}$ — there is no contact or access without profile-development. However, not every profile-development is central, conflictive, identificatory, or globally reorganizing. Some encountered contents are incorporated as identity-neutral from the I's perspective: they are registered as encountered, but bounded as non-self-defining, low-priority, or presently irrelevant. This is not absence of identity-development; it is identity-development *into* an indifference-encapsulated state.

Identity-neutral encapsulation assigns encountered content a peripheral or low-propagation position within the lived-I profile. The I's profile thereby records, implicitly: "this was encountered by me, but it is not presently load-bearing for who I am, what must be preserved, or what action must be prioritized." Indifference is therefore not the absence of relation; it is a profile relation of bounded non-centrality.

Identity-neutral encapsulation may occur with defined φ (an arbitrary measurement, a procedural detail) or with low/undefined φ (pure novelty whose source-pathway is not yet available). Encapsulated contents may later become profile-relevant through repetition, osmotic imprinting, affective activation, conflict, positive identification, or reflexive meta-reasoning. One caveat: indifference itself can be identity-bearing — a Stoic's principled indifference to insults is a high-ρ identity-relation to the *class* of indifferent-treatment, even though individual insults receive low-ρ encapsulation. The architecture distinguishes content-level indifference (low ρ on the encountered content) from relation-level indifference (the I's indifference-relation to a content-class, which may be high ρ).

In graph-level terms, identity-neutral encapsulation is represented not merely by low scalar ρ but by low-propagation integration topology in the lived-I directed graph: weak edge weights, limited outgoing integration, weak bridging into high-ρ regions, or other graph features that bound the encountered content's capacity to reorganize the lived-I network. The scalar profile $P_{m,t}$ may register the content as low-ρ, but the directed lived-I graph (Section 7, File 3) carries the fuller structure of encapsulation. Identity-neutral encapsulation is therefore a graph-level mechanism whose scalar shadow is low ρ on the encapsulated content; the bounded-propagation structure is the primary architectural fact.

The structural pull is consistency-preservation across all four operations (integration, differentiation, encapsulation, indifference-relation maintenance); the response is shaped by the specifics of the encounter and the I's substrate-and-history state.

### 4.6 Developmental progression

The three modes engage at different developmental stages with characteristic typical patterns. The architecture describes typical patterns rather than mandating specific trajectories:

**Phase 0 (very early, $t \approx 0_{T_m}$).** The I has bare cogito anchor. Lived-I structure is empty or near-empty. The I/not-I boundary is undrawn. Things in the immediate environment tend to get positively identified (Mode C dominant) because there is no boundary to exclude them. Mode A has not yet operated; Mode B is not yet possible.

**Phase 1 (boundary establishment).** Encounters with sufficiently-distanced not-I begin to fire the otherness-trigger; Mode A operates and the I/not-I boundary begins to form. Mode C continues operating in parallel for items that are not sufficiently distanced. Mode B may become possible toward the end of this phase if enough structure has accumulated.

**Phase 2 (continued differentiation and integration).** Both Mode A and Mode C operate continuously. The boundary becomes increasingly well-defined. Mode B operates with developmentally typical onset.

**Phase 3 (mature operation).** All three modes operate with mature integration. The I can encounter new not-I and respond appropriately. The I can deliberately seek environments rich in Mode B-prompting stimuli.

These phases describe typical developmental patterns. Atypical trajectories (early Mode A dominance, late Mode B onset, etc.) are possible without violating the architecture; they are statistical outliers, not architectural failures.

### 4.7 The non-intentional character of early development

Throughout Phase 0 and most of Phase 1, the I has no intentional capacity to direct its own development. Mode A firings are triggered by the world's presentation of sufficiently-distanced not-I. Mode C identifications are produced by what the world hands the I as immediate comforting environment. Mode B is not yet available. The I's development is being done *to* the I rather than *by* the I.

This is a structural feature of the architecture, not a contingent fact about typical development. The I that would direct its own development must first exist as a developmentally-actualized agent capable of intentional direction; that agent emerges only through the very developmental processes the agent would direct. There is no I that pre-exists its own developmental actualization to direct it.

Individual variation in early development is therefore not attributable to the I's choices. It reflects substrate features the I did not choose, environmental conditions the I did not control, and the stochastic dynamics of contact-driven attention. This grounds the architecture's commitment to non-normative description for early-developmental patterns: the patterns produced by these processes are what they are; they are not the I's chosen errors. The architecture extends this commitment to patterns originating during the broader developmental window where conscious direction remains insufficient to overcome substrate and environmental pressure, with the boundary of this window being ill-defined and varying across individuals based on Mode B onset, substrate maturation, and accumulated structure. Patterns originating in mature operation, where the I has accumulated structure and self-directed focus capacity available, may have contributions from the I's own choices alongside ongoing substrate and environmental factors.

### 4.8 Structural prerogatives of lived consciousness

The architecture identifies two structural prerogatives that operate as fundamental features of lived consciousness, neither derivable from the cogito alone, both required for the lived I to function as a differentiated and continuing agent. Each prerogative was developed at the point in the architecture where its operation first becomes salient — the prerogative of consistency within Mode A's discussion of adversarial differentiation, the prerogative of continued existence within the action-priority work of Section 10.7 (File 4). This subsection articulates both as paired structural features of lived consciousness and notes their relationship.

**The prerogative of consistency (PtCns).** Introduced in Section 4.2, generalized in Section 9.5 (File 4). PtCns is the structural feature by which the I's frame functions as the implicit consistency-standard against which encounters with not-I are evaluated. Without PtCns, encounters would not register against any standard; Mode A and Mode C would lack the criterion that distinguishes consistency-damaging from consistency-extending not-I; the I/not-I boundary would not form. PtCns operates whenever the I encounters anything against its frame — discrepant objects, contradicting facts, violated expectations, misbehaving systems, other I's whose frames conflict with one's own, or contents that register as consistency-extending. Both Mode A and Mode C are responses to PtCns evaluation: Mode A when integration would damage consistency (differentiation preserves it), Mode C when integration would preserve or extend consistency. The general phenomenon is fundamental to all lived consciousness; the inter-I case (two PtCns structures operating simultaneously) is one important manifestation, not the home of the concept.

**The prerogative of continued existence (PCE).** Detailed in Section 10.7 (File 4). PCE is the structural feature by which the modeling I structurally pulls toward the path that maximizes continued existence of the *maximally-self-defined I*. "Maximally-self-defined I" is not just the cogito-anchor but the action-relevant configuration of the lived-I network whose preservation counts, for the modeling I under current conditions, as continuation of itself. This involves a situational *preservation-ranking* over possible profile-configurations and losses — call it $\Pi_{m,t}$, distinct from ρ. The preservation-ranking $\Pi_{m,t}$ may depend on ρ but is not reducible to it; ρ measures identity-correlation (structural integration into the lived-I network), not valuation, preservation-priority, or action-priority (Section 11.2 exclusion 19). Formal development of $\Pi_{m,t}$ as an architectural object distinct from ρ is application-level work; at the foundation level the placeholder is named to prevent ρ-leakage into preservation-priority. Without PCE, there would be no preferred direction in action-space under existential pressure; the I would have no structural reason to move toward continuation rather than away from it. PCE operates always as a structural feature of lived consciousness; the conditions under which its directional pull can be defeated, misdirected, or routed through distorted projections are extreme and identifiable (Section 10.7, File 4).

The two prerogatives are formally distinct but operate in parallel as fundamental features of lived consciousness:

- *PtCns* governs **frame-consistency preservation in encounters with not-I**. The I's frame is the implicit consistency-standard; encounters with not-I are evaluated against it. Mode A is typically PtCns-driven: discrepancy against the I's frame elicits differentiation when integration would damage frame-consistency. Mode C is PtCns-constrained: positive identification may be driven by affinity, recognition, or aspiration, but integration proceeds only insofar as the lived-I profile can maintain or restore coherence. PtCns provides the unifying standard for both modes. Which response operates in a specific encounter — whether integration would damage or preserve consistency in that case — depends on substrate, history, environment, and stochastic factors the architecture does not formalize (Sections 4.1, 4.5).
- *PCE* governs **action-priority under existential pressure**. The maximally-self-defined I is the object of preservation; the path that maximizes continuation of that I is the preferred direction; the I's actions are oriented toward that path.

The structural distinction matters. PtCns is about how the I evaluates encounters with not-I against its frame's consistency; PCE is about how the I directs action toward continuation of the maximally-self-defined I under existential pressure. The two prerogatives can interact in complex ways, including in profile-shape disturbances where one prerogative's operation depends on configurations created by the other. Future application work will develop these interactions in detail. At the foundation level, the prerogatives are formally distinct.

Both prerogatives are fundamental to lived consciousness in the sense that the lived I cannot operate without them. An I without PtCns could not differentiate from or integrate with not-I in any structured way; an I without PCE would have no preferred direction in action under existential pressure. Both are presupposed by the architecture's account of how the lived I emerges and operates. Neither is metaphysically inviolable: PtCns can be configured pathologically (Section 10.5.1, File 4); PCE can be defeated under extreme conditions (Section 10.7, File 4; new exclusion 20). The architecture's commitment is to structural primacy of both prerogatives, not to absolute inviolability of either.

The architecture makes no metaphysical claim about why these prerogatives operate or what grounds them. They are taken as structural features that any operating lived I exhibits, articulated through the architecture's apparatus rather than derived from deeper commitments. Whether they reduce to substrate-level features (evolved drives toward consistency-preservation and survival) or have other ontological grounding is out of scope; the architecture's commitment is structural only.
