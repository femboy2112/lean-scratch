## Abstract

This paper applies the Two-Layer Identity-Correlation Architecture (Files 1-5) to temporal phenomenology. Using only the foundation's existing apparatus — substrate-bound focus capacity, accumulating verification tools and lived-I structure, focus dynamics, the three modes of I-development, the persistence relation, and the contact interface — the paper derives structural accounts of subjective time-flow rate, the structure of the felt "now," and the past/future asymmetry. No new architectural commitments are introduced; the orders of imprinting and the existing coordinates suffice. The architecture predicts subjective time-acceleration with age (with sublinear scaling preventing the frame-lag absurdity), characteristic phenomenological signatures for novelty, routine, danger, and flow, the felt thickness and "now-ness" of the present as a function of focus on present-imprinting content, and the past/future asymmetry as the structural difference between actual imprinting traces (persisted) and Mode B projection of possible imprinting chains. Several phenomena remain explicitly open: the felt continuous-stream character of experience itself, dreams and altered states, and the physics-level direction of time, which the architecture treats as outside its scope.

## 1. Introduction

Temporal phenomenology is among the phenomena where philosophical accounts of consciousness most often struggle. The felt acceleration of subjective time with age, the structure of the specious present, the asymmetry between memory and anticipation, the time-distortion of flow states and danger, and the felt directional flow of experience all resist single-mechanism explanations. This paper shows that the Two-Layer Identity-Correlation Architecture, without modification, derives structural accounts of several of these phenomena from foundational commitments already in place.

The application is intended as a worked demonstration that the architecture has explanatory reach beyond what it was originally built to address. The foundation does not posit anything specifically about temporal experience; the temporal-phenomenology results follow from the architecture's existing apparatus when that apparatus is asked questions about subjective time. This is the kind of result a structural foundation should produce.

Section 2 lists the architectural prerequisites the paper uses. Section 3 derives the account of subjective time-flow rate, including the time-acceleration phenomenology, sublinear scaling that prevents extreme frame-lag, and characteristic time-distortions in novelty, routine, danger, and flow. Section 4 develops the structure of "now" as the imprinting-interface where not-I → I interaction is currently active, with felt "now-ness" as a function of focus on present-imprinting content. Section 5 derives the past/future asymmetry from the structural difference between actual imprinting (persisted) and Mode B projection (possible). Section 6 lists predictions and exclusions. Section 7 lists open problems and phenomena the application explicitly does not address.

## 2. Architectural prerequisites

This section lists the foundational commitments from Files 1-5 that the temporal-phenomenology account uses. A reader thoroughly familiar with the foundation may skip directly to Section 3.

### 2.1 Substrate-bound focus capacity (from File 3, Section 8.1)

The architecture posits a focus capacity $M_m(t)$ determined by substrate, which the modeling I cannot choose:

$$M_m(t) = \Gamma^M_m(\mathsf{S}_m, t), \qquad \hat\iota_m \not\to M_m(t).$$

Focus allocation is bounded:

$$|\mathsf{Foc}^m_t| \leq M_m(t).$$

Crucially for temporal phenomenology: $M_m(t)$ is roughly constant across mature adulthood (modulo substrate degradation). The I cannot expand its own bandwidth; the substrate caps how much can be processed simultaneously.

### 2.2 Two-phase focus shift (from File 3, Section 8.2)

Focus allocation has contact-driven and self-directed components:

$$\mathsf{Foc}^m_t = \mathsf{Foc}^m_t[\text{contact-driven}] \oplus \mathsf{Foc}^m_t[\text{self-directed}].$$

In early development, the second term is empty; focus is captured exogenously by salience. As the lived-I network accumulates, self-directed focus grows; the transition is gradual and never complete.

### 2.3 The contact interface (from File 1, Section 2.5)

The contact interface $\chi_m: T_m \times \mathsf{N}_m \to [0, 1]$ is time-indexed. $\chi_m(t, n)$ is the contact value between the modeling I and not-I item $n$ at time $t$. The interface is part of the substrate $\mathsf{S}_m = (\mathsf{B}_m, \mathsf{E}_m, \chi_m)$.

### 2.4 Imprinting events occur at present time (from File 3, Section 8.4)

Imprinting events have form $e = (s, u, v, k, \alpha, \beta)$, time-stamped by their occurrence-time $s$. At any time $s$, only events with timestamp $s$ are happening; events at other times are either historical (persisted via $\sim^m_H$) or projected (cognitively simulated).

### 2.5 Persistence relation (from File 3, Section 6.2)

The persistence equivalence relation $\sim^m_H$ links time-stamped content tokens across time. The historical content domain is the quotient:

$$\mathsf{H}^m_t := \widetilde{\mathsf{H}}^m_t / \sim^m_H, \qquad \widetilde{\mathsf{H}}^m_t = \{(s, x) : s \leq_m t, x \in \mathsf{A}^m_s\}.$$

Past events have actual imprinting traces; the persistence relation is what gives the I access to its own past via the historical content domain.

### 2.6 Mode B and meta-cognition (from File 2, Section 4.4)

Mode B is the I's spontaneous meta-reasoning capacity, operating on accumulated structure. Once Mode B is available, the I can take its own contents, reasoning, and integrations as objects of further attention. Mode B also operates on possibility-space — the I projects possible scenarios, simulates counterfactuals, anticipates future events.

Mode B is not available at $t = 0_{T_m}$ (no accumulated structure to attend to). Its onset follows only the architecture-level constraints stated in File 2 Section 4.4: zero at origin, non-zero later where substrate and environment support meta-reasoning, and variable across cogniting beings. No specific distributional shape (Gaussian, bell-curve, or otherwise) is implied by the foundation; the distribution across cogniting beings is empirical and outside the foundation's predictive scope.

### 2.7 Imprinting pathways (from File 3, Section 8.5)

Contents reach the I through three characteristic pathway-orders:

- First-order ($D=1$): minimal-mediation salience capture through substrate.
- Second-order ($D=2$): cognitive-substrate-mediated thoughts.
- Third-order ($D=3$): somatic-then-cognitive-mediated emotions and sensations.

Each substrate-mediation stage involves lossy compression. Higher-order content tends to have lower explicit propositional information density.

### 2.8 The verification toolset and lived-I network (from Files 2, 3, 4)

The verification toolset $\mathsf{Tools}^m_t$ accumulates over development through verification-tool imprinting. The lived-I network $\mathsf{L}^{m,k}_t$ accumulates through content imprinting. Both grow over the I's development, producing the accumulated structure that integration must operate against.

## 3. Subjective time-flow rate

This section develops the architecture's account of why subjective time appears to accelerate with age, why novelty stretches time and routine compresses it, why danger produces time-dilation and flow produces time-disappearance, and why the apparent acceleration does not produce frame-lag at extreme ages.

### 3.1 The bandwidth-bounded integration argument

The architecture commits to substrate-bound focus capacity $M_m(t)$ that the I cannot choose, and to accumulating verification tools and lived-I structure over development. From these commitments alone, the architecture derives a prediction about temporal phenomenology.

Consider what processing the I performs at any moment. The I's focus is allocated to current contents; current contents must be integrated with the I's accumulated structure (verification toolset, lived-I network, prior content); the bandwidth available for this combined processing is bounded by $M_m(t)$.

As accumulated structure grows, integration draws more bandwidth. The architecture commits to bandwidth being substrate-bound and roughly constant in adulthood. Therefore, as accumulated structure grows, less bandwidth is available for raw input processing — or each "frame" of conscious processing takes longer because more integration work must be performed per frame, or both.

Either formulation produces the same phenomenological prediction: subjective time accelerates with age. The same clock-interval contains less raw novel content per subjective frame, or fewer subjective frames per clock-interval, or both. Clock-time appears to flow faster relative to subjective experience.

### 3.2 Two formulations, both probably operating

*Formulation 1 — Frame-rate compression.* Each "frame" of conscious processing must integrate raw content with accumulated structure. As structure grows, processing time per frame grows. With fixed bandwidth, frames per clock-second decrease. Subjective frames per clock-second decrease, so clock-time appears to flow faster.

*Formulation 2 — Per-frame allocation shift.* Bandwidth is fixed but partitioned within each frame between raw input processing and integration with structure:

$$\mathsf{Foc}^m_t = \mathsf{Foc}^{m,\text{in}}_t \sqcup \mathsf{Foc}^{m,\text{int}}_t,$$

with constraint $|\mathsf{Foc}^{m,\text{in}}_t| + |\mathsf{Foc}^{m,\text{int}}_t| \leq M_m(t)$. As $\mathsf{Tools}^m_t$ and $\mathsf{L}^m_t$ grow, $|\mathsf{Foc}^{m,\text{int}}_t|$ grows because there's more structure to integrate against. With fixed $M_m(t)$, raw input bandwidth shrinks relative to total.

These formulations give similar phenomenological predictions but commit to different mechanisms. In practice both probably operate: frame processing time may grow somewhat AND raw content per frame may decrease. The architecture does not commit to one formulation over the other without further empirical work. The minimum commitment that licenses the phenomenology is just that bandwidth is bounded, integration consumes bandwidth, and accumulated structure grows.

### 3.3 Sublinear scaling prevents extreme frame-lag

If integration cost scaled linearly with accumulated structure, very old people would experience extreme frame lag — cognitive slowness as their lifetime structure pile-up dominated bandwidth. Empirically this isn't what happens. Most subjective time-acceleration occurs between roughly young adulthood and middle age, with the rate stabilizing in older age (substrate degradation produces a different phenomenology than the bandwidth-overload prediction would).

Sublinear scaling — logarithmic or otherwise — fits the phenomenology better. Why might integration cost scale sublinearly? Several plausible reasons within the architecture:

- *Higher-level abstractions compress lower-level detail.* As verification tools and lived-I structure accumulate, higher-level patterns become available that subsume many lower-level patterns. Integration cost per new content operates against compressed higher-level structure rather than summing over all prior content.
- *Pattern-recognition becomes more efficient with accumulation.* The marginal integration cost of a new instance of a familiar pattern is much lower than the cost of integrating a fully novel pattern.
- *Memory consolidation and forgetting reduce structural load.* Sleep, ordinary forgetting, and consolidation processes condense accumulated structure rather than allowing unbounded growth.
- *The three-tier structure naturally produces compression hierarchy.* The cogito sits at fixed structural position; verification tools build on it; higher-level abstractions build on tools. Lower levels get more efficient integration via higher levels.

Probably all of these operate together. The exact functional form is application-level; what matters is that scaling is sublinear, which the phenomenology and the bandwidth argument both demand.

### 3.4 Predictions about subjective time-flow

The bandwidth-bounded integration account, with sublinear scaling, produces several predictions:

*Subjective time acceleration with age.* As accumulated structure grows, integration draws more bandwidth, raw content processing shrinks, frames per clock-second decrease, or both. The same clock-interval contains less subjectively-novel content. Importantly, the prediction is monotonic in *accumulated lived-I structure*, not just in chronological age — someone with rich novel experiences in middle age should show less acceleration than someone in deep routine, even at the same chronological age.

*Time dilation in novelty.* Novel situations have less existing structure to integrate against, so integration draw is smaller and raw input processing can be larger. More raw content per frame, slower subjective time. This matches the phenomenology of vacations feeling longer than equivalent home time, of unfamiliar cities feeling temporally rich.

*Time compression in routine.* Routine activates extensive integration with existing structure. Integration draw dominates, raw input is small. Less raw content per frame, faster subjective time. Routine days feel short.

*Time dilation in danger or extreme salience.* Threat commandeers focus toward incoming content via the salience-gating substrate prioritizing first-order pathway. Raw input processing dominates; integration is suppressed for non-threat-relevant structure. More raw content per frame, slower subjective time. Matches the phenomenology of accidents feeling slow-motion.

*Time disappearing in flow.* Flow states involve high integration with task-relevant structure but suppression of meta-monitoring (including time-tracking). The integration dominates focus but the meta-tracking-of-passage is suppressed. Subjective time-of-passing is not registered, even though clock time elapses normally. Different mechanism than acceleration but compatible with the bandwidth account: meta-tracking would require Mode B operation on time-passage as content, which is suppressed during deep absorption.

*Differential subjective duration across I's for same clock interval.* Different I's with different accumulated structures should experience the same clock-interval differently. Children's hours are subjectively longer than adults' hours, even excluding novelty effects. The architecture predicts this directly.

### 3.5 The 10-minutes-over-lifespan observation

The observation that "ten minutes at age five contains more new than ten minutes at age fifty" captures a real phenomenology. There's an arithmetic version (10 minutes is a smaller fraction of a longer lifespan) but the deeper structural account predicts the asymmetry isn't only because there's more clock-lifespan to compare against, but because the integration-content of those 10 minutes has shifted.

Specifically: 10 minutes at age 5 has small integration draw (little accumulated structure to integrate against) and large raw input processing. 10 minutes at age 50 has large integration draw (extensive accumulated structure) and small raw input processing. Same clock duration, different subjective duration, because different ratios of raw-input to integration in the bandwidth split.

## 4. The structure of "now"

The felt present is not just "the current moment" abstractly. It has structure — a felt thickness, a quality of being-now that some moments have more strongly than others, a relationship to the just-past and the about-to-happen. This section develops the architecture's account.

### 4.1 "Now" as the imprinting-interface

"Now" has structural privilege as the time-coordinate at which the not-I → I imprinting interface is currently active. This is where actual imprinting events occur. Past time *had* the interface; future time *might* have it; only present time *has* it active.

In architecture terms: imprinting events $e = (s, u, v, k, \alpha, \beta)$ are time-stamped. At any time $s$, only events with timestamp $s$ are happening. The contact interface $\chi_m(t, n)$ at the present time is what supports actual not-I → I interaction. Past values of $\chi_m$ are stored via persistence; future values exist only as cognitive projections.

This makes "now" architecturally privileged: it is the unique time when the I's lived structure can change. Past doesn't change (modulo memory reconsolidation, which is itself a present-time event operating on persistence relations). Future doesn't change because it hasn't happened. Only present-time supports actual imprinting events that update $G^{m,k}_t$, $\mathsf{Tools}^m_t$, and the lived-I network.

This specification doesn't introduce new architectural machinery; it makes explicit a structural feature implicit in the existing time-indexing throughout the architecture.

### 4.2 Felt "now-ness" as focus on present-imprinting content

The felt quality of "now-ness" — the strength with which the present feels present — is a function of focus on present-imprinting content.

When focus is engaged with contents currently being imprinted (high κ from current contact, source maps tracing to active not-I impingement), "now" feels strong. The I's experience is anchored in present imprinting flow.

When focus drifts — to memory contents (high ρ but low present κ for the original source), to imagination (cognitive projections via Mode B), to internal rumination (Mode B operating on accumulated structure rather than current input) — present-time imprinting flow continues, but the I isn't attending to it. "Now-ness" weakens because the I isn't attending to what's actually now.

This explains the phenomenology of "spaced out" states: focus has drifted from contents currently being imprinted to contents whose imprinting is not currently at the interface. The clock is still moving, the world is still impinging, but the I's experience of being-here-now is attenuated.

### 4.3 The specious present: retention, impressional moment, protention

Phenomenologically, "now" has structure beyond mere existence. The felt present typically encompasses about 2-3 seconds of integrated time — wide enough to integrate a sentence, hold a melody, but narrow enough to feel like "the present" rather than "the past." Husserl's tripartite structure (retention of the just-past, primal impression of the impressional moment, protention of the about-to-come) describes this thickness.

In architecture terms, the specious present is the simultaneous focus-allocation across:

- *Retention*: contents whose imprinting just occurred, still held in current focus. Recently-imprinted content persists in focus for some duration before being released; while in focus, it contributes to felt-present structure.
- *Impressional moment*: contents whose imprinting is happening now via current contact. These are the items at the active interface, with high κ, currently undergoing $\sigma^m_t$ updates.
- *Protention*: Mode B projection of imminent next content. The cognitive substrate produces short-horizon predictions about what's about to be imprinted; these projections are held in focus alongside actual current content.

The breadth of "now" is the breadth of this composite focus — how much of each component the I holds simultaneously. Sharp focus on a specific content (high concentration on one thing) gives narrow "now"; broad receptive focus (open attention to everything) gives wider "now"; drifting focus (attention elsewhere) gives diffuse or absent "now-ness."

Bandwidth is what bounds the breadth: the I can hold only so much in focus simultaneously, where "so much" is bounded by $M_m(t)$. In an I with rich accumulated structure (mature adulthood), more of $M_m(t)$ goes to integration; less is available for the impressional + retention + protention composite. The specious present may therefore have characteristic age-related variation, narrower in later life as integration draws more bandwidth.

### 4.4 Why focus drift weakens "now-ness" — fuller derivation

Combining the imprinting-interface specification (4.1) with the focus-on-present-imprinting account (4.2): focus-drift weakens "now-ness" because the I's attention moves to contents whose imprinting is not currently happening at the interface.

Memory contents have ρ but not current κ. Their imprinting occurred in the past; their persistence relation links them to the I's current structure, but the active imprinting of those contents is not happening now. Focus on memory engages persistence-relation traces, not current imprinting events.

Imagination contents are Mode B projections; their "imprinting" is cognitive simulation, not actual not-I → I interaction. Focus on imagination engages cognitive substrate operations on possibility-space, not active imprinting at the interface.

Internal rumination is Mode B operating on the I's own accumulated structure. Focus on rumination engages already-accumulated material, not current imprinting.

In each case, the I is focused on contents whose imprinting is not at the active interface. Present-time imprinting still occurs (the contact interface is still active; the world is still impinging on the substrate), but the I's experience is anchored elsewhere. "Now-ness" weakens because the I isn't attending to what's actually now.

This produces the felt phenomenology of spaced-out states, daydreaming, dissociation: the present moment continues clock-wise, but the I's relationship to the present is attenuated because focus has migrated away from contents currently being imprinted.

### 4.5 Connection to the bandwidth account

The "now" account and the bandwidth-acceleration account from Section 3 are connected. Subjective time-acceleration with age affects "now" in two ways:

First, the breadth of the felt present may narrow. As integration draw grows with accumulated structure, less bandwidth is available for the retention + impressional + protention composite. The specious present may become narrower or thinner with age, contributing to the felt acceleration.

Second, more cognitive content arises from accumulated structure rather than from current input. Even when the I attempts to attend to the present, more of the bandwidth goes to integrating present input with extensive prior structure — meaning the felt present is shaped more by what one already knows and less by what is being received. This may contribute to the subjective sense that the world feels familiar or repetitive in older age, even when objectively novel content is present.

## 5. Past and future as actual versus projected imprinting

The architecture explains the phenomenological asymmetry between memory and anticipation through the structural difference between actual imprinting (persisted) and Mode B projection (possible).

### 5.1 The structural distinction

Past content has actual source-attribution traces. The not-I → substrate → I chain occurred; ρ-integration formed from real events; the persistence relation $\sim^m_H$ links current content-tokens to actual prior content-tokens. The I has actual record.

Future content has no actual source-attribution. It is cognitive substrate projection — Mode B operating on possibility-space, generating "what an imprinting chain *might* look like" rather than "what an imprinting chain *did* look like."

This is the core distinction. Past imprinting happened; its traces are in the persistence relation and the historical content domain. Future imprinting hasn't happened; it exists only as cognitive simulation in the substrate.

### 5.2 Phenomenological consequences

The phenomenological asymmetries follow:

- *Past feels concrete because there is actual integration history.* Memory contents have ρ-values from real prior imprinting; the lived-I network has been shaped by actual events. The felt concreteness of memory derives from the actuality of its source traces.
- *Future feels less concrete because there is no integration history yet.* Anticipated contents have projected ρ-values from imagined future imprinting; the lived-I network hasn't been shaped by them. Anticipation lacks the felt weight of actuality.
- *Anticipated emotions are typically weaker than remembered emotions.* Third-order pathway dynamics (Section 8.5, File 3) operate on actual somatic states for memory (sometimes re-activating originals); for anticipation they operate on cognitive projections of somatic states, with weaker activation. The body responds to actual history more strongly than to projected possibility.
- *Past feels "behind" while future feels "ahead."* The persistence relation orders past imprintings; meta-cognitive projection extends forward. The directionality is structural — actual events point backward in the persistence chain; possible events point forward in projection space.

### 5.3 Memory and Mode B

Memory is not pure persistence-recall; it involves Mode B operating on persisted content. The I attends to a memory; Mode B engages with it; the memory may be re-integrated, reconsolidated, or revised. The architecture's commitment is that the *original* imprinting was actual (occurred at a past time-coordinate via real not-I → I interaction); the *current attention to* the memory is Mode B operating on the persisted trace.

This explains why memory feels concrete (its source is actual imprinting) but also revisable (Mode B can re-attend, re-integrate, reconsolidate). Memory phenomenology is the conjunction of persistence and meta-cognition.

### 5.4 Anticipation and Mode B

Anticipation is Mode B operating purely on possibility-space. The I projects future scenarios using accumulated patterns from past imprintings, generating "what an imprinting chain might look like" without any actual imprinting having occurred. The cognitive substrate runs simulations.

Because anticipation operates only on cognitive projection (no actual past source-traces), its content has no persistence-relation grounding. There is no $\sim^m_H$-trace from a future event to the present, because the future event hasn't occurred. The architecture makes this asymmetry structural, not contingent.

When anticipation triggers third-order pathway dynamics — anticipated fear, anticipated grief, anticipatory anxiety — the somatic activation is real (the body responds to the projection as if the event were occurring), but the activation is driven by cognitive simulation rather than by actual current contact with the projected event. This is why anticipated emotions typically have lower intensity than remembered emotions of comparable events: the somatic system responds to actuality more strongly than to simulation.

### 5.5 The directionality

Past feels "behind" and future feels "ahead" because of the structural difference between actual persisted imprinting and projected possibility:

- The persistence relation $\sim^m_H$ orders past time-coordinates. The chain extends backward from the present through actual events.
- Mode B projection extends forward into possibility-space. The I projects what *could* happen next, what *could* happen later.
- The asymmetry is in the structural status of the temporal relations, not in time itself.

What the architecture explains is the felt directionality of temporal experience. Why memory feels concrete and oriented backward; why anticipation feels less concrete and oriented forward. The asymmetry of time experience falls out of the asymmetry between actual past imprinting and possible future imprinting.

### 5.6 The direction of time itself is a physics problem

The architecture takes no position on why time itself has a direction at the physical level (entropy, causal structure, the arrow given by thermodynamic asymmetry). These are physics problems, not phenomenology problems. The architecture explains why temporal *experience* has the asymmetry it does — the structural difference between actual imprinting and Mode B projection — without needing to derive temporal direction from anything more basic.

This is appropriate scope-limitation. A foundation paper on the phenomenology of time should explain the felt asymmetry; it should not claim to settle questions about the physical arrow of time. The architecture's commitment is just that there's an asymmetry between actual past imprinting and projected future possibility, and this asymmetry has the structural form the architecture specifies.

## 6. Predictions and exclusions

### 6.1 Specific predictions

The temporal-phenomenology account makes the following predictions:

**P1. Subjective time accelerates monotonically with accumulated lived-I structure** (not chronologically with age alone). I's with rich novel experiences in middle age should show less acceleration than I's in deep routine, controlling for chronological age. Verification toolset richness, lived-I network breadth, and active integration-load are the relevant variables.

**P2. Subjective time dilates in novelty.** Unfamiliar situations, new environments, and content with little existing structure to integrate against produce subjectively-longer durations.

**P3. Subjective time compresses in routine.** Familiar situations with extensive integration-structure produce subjectively-shorter durations.

**P4. Subjective time dilates in danger or extreme salience.** Threat-conditions producing first-order salience-gating dominance produce subjectively-longer durations.

**P5. Subjective time disappears in flow.** Deep absorption with suppressed meta-tracking produces unmeasured subjective duration; clock-time elapses normally but the I doesn't experience it as elapsing.

**P6. Felt "now-ness" is a function of focus on present-imprinting content.** Sharp focus on contents currently being imprinted produces strong "now-ness"; focus on memory, imagination, or rumination produces weakened "now-ness."

**P7. The breadth of the specious present varies with focus and accumulated structure.** Broad receptive focus widens the felt present; sharp absorption narrows it; older I's may have characteristically narrower specious presents due to integration draw.

**P8. Memory feels more concrete than anticipation.** Persisted imprinting traces produce felt concreteness; Mode B projections produce felt possibility without weight.

**P9. Anticipated emotions are typically weaker than remembered emotions of comparable events.** Somatic systems respond to actual contact more strongly than to cognitive simulation.

**P10. Past and future feel directionally oriented (backward and forward respectively).** The directionality is structural, derived from persistence-relation chain (backward) and Mode B projection (forward).

**P11. Sublinear scaling of integration cost prevents extreme frame-lag at extreme ages.** Acceleration stabilizes; older adults do not experience cognitive slowdown of the form predicted by linear scaling.

**P12. Substrate degradation (in dementia, severe illness, severe cognitive decline) produces a different phenomenology than the bandwidth-overload prediction.** The architecture predicts substrate degradation reduces $M_m(t)$ itself, producing reduced overall capacity rather than the specific time-acceleration signature.

### 6.2 Exclusions

The temporal-phenomenology application explicitly does not claim to explain:

**E1. The felt continuous-stream character of experience itself.** Why does experience feel like a continuous flow rather than as discrete frames? The architecture is consistent with both continuous and discretized readings of imprinting dynamics; the felt continuity is not derived from substrate operation.

**E2. The metaphysics of time.** The architecture posits time as part of primal not-I (Section 2.5, File 1) but does not address what time fundamentally is.

**E3. The physical arrow of time.** Why thermodynamics has an arrow, why causation is asymmetric, why entropy increases — these are physics problems, outside the architecture's scope.

**E4. Dreams and altered states.** Sleep, dreaming, anesthesia, intoxication, meditation, psychedelic experience all involve transformed temporal phenomenology that the architecture's apparatus might handle (different bandwidth allocation patterns, different focus dynamics, different mode operation) but does not currently address. These deserve focused attention rather than off-the-cuff treatment; they are work for subsequent application papers.

**E5. The metaphysics of memory.** Whether memory is direct retrieval, reconstructive, dispositional, etc., is not specified by the architecture beyond the persistence-relation and Mode B operation. The architecture is consistent with multiple memory metaphysics.

**E6. The metaphysics of anticipation.** Whether anticipation involves genuine simulation, default predictive processing, model-based imagination, etc., is not specified by the architecture beyond Mode B operation on possibility-space.

**E7. Time perception in non-human cogniting beings.** The architecture commits to form-invariance conditional on existence (other cogniting beings have the same architectural form), but the empirical predictions about subjective time-flow rate may differ for cogniting beings with different substrate-mediation patterns, different verification-tool acquisition rates, or different bandwidth profiles.

## 7. Open problems

Several questions remain for further work:

**The exact functional form of integration-cost scaling.** The architecture predicts sublinear scaling but does not specify whether it is logarithmic, polynomial-with-fractional-exponent, or otherwise. Different functional forms make different empirical predictions about the rate of subjective time-acceleration across the lifespan and the asymptotic behavior in late life.

**The relative contribution of frame-rate compression versus per-frame allocation shift.** Both formulations from Section 3.2 give similar predictions in the basic acceleration case but may diverge in specific predictions (e.g., during high cognitive load, during meditation, in different age groups). Empirical work could discriminate.

**The structural account of dreams.** Dream-time is wildly variable — sometimes compressed, sometimes dilated, sometimes nonlinear. Dreams probably involve transformed κ-φ-ρ patterns (low contact with primal not-I, high contact with internally-generated content, distorted φ, often-preserved or hyperactive ρ), altered focus dynamics (less self-directed focus, more contact-driven within dream-content), and specific Mode B patterns (lucid dreaming as Mode B operating within dream-state). A focused application paper on dreams would need to develop this carefully.

**Altered states more generally.** Anesthesia, intoxication, meditation (particularly long retreats, where subjective time-distortion is well-documented), psychedelic states, and hypnagogic states all involve characteristic temporal phenomenology that the architecture should ultimately be able to characterize. Each probably involves specific transformations of bandwidth allocation, focus dynamics, and mode operation. These deserve separate treatment.

**The phenomenology of waiting.** Waiting for a known event has characteristic phenomenology — time often feels to drag despite the I knowing when the event will occur. This involves Mode B projection with high anticipatory engagement combined with low first-order salience capture. The architecture's apparatus suggests an account but it has not been worked out.

**The phenomenology of boredom.** Boredom involves low first-order salience capture combined with low self-directed focus engagement. Time feels both stretched and empty. The architecture's apparatus suggests a structural account but it has not been developed.

**The phenomenology of time-travel intuitions.** Memory-like vividness of imagined future scenarios, "phantom" memories of events that didn't occur, déjà vu, jamais vu, and related phenomena involve specific patterns of persistence-relation, Mode B operation, and source-attribution that the architecture's apparatus could handle but has not addressed.

**The compression characteristics of specific substrate components.** The general lossy-compression observation does not specify what features each substrate component preserves and which it loses. This connects to the broader open problem (Section 13, File 5) about the compression characteristics of specific substrate components.

**Variation in subjective-time across I's with different cognitive profiles.** The architecture predicts variation but does not specify the dimensions in detail. Empirical work on attention-deficit profiles, autism-spectrum profiles, depression states, and other cognitive variations could surface dimensions the architecture should address.

**The integration of temporal phenomenology with narrative self.** The narrative self-phenomenon (the I's autobiographical-narrative sense of itself as a continuous protagonist with a history) connects to the persistence-relation and Mode B operating on past content, but the integration with temporal phenomenology is not yet worked out. A future application paper on narrative self could address this.

## 8. Conclusion

This application paper has demonstrated that the Two-Layer Identity-Correlation Architecture, without modification, derives structural accounts of subjective time-flow rate, the structure of "now," and the past/future asymmetry from foundational commitments already in place. The architecture's bandwidth-bounded focus capacity, accumulating verification tools and lived-I structure, focus dynamics, three modes of I-development, persistence relation, contact interface, and orders of imprinting are sufficient. No new architectural commitments are introduced.

The result is a worked demonstration that the foundation has explanatory reach beyond what it was originally built to address. The methodological critiques of the architecture (which characterized it as iteratively coherence-fitted to its own dialectical history) are at least partially answered by this kind of result: a phenomenon the architecture wasn't built to address, where the architecture's commitments produce specific predictive accounts, with predictions matching widely-reported phenomenology. The architecture is doing the explanatory work foundations are supposed to do.

The temporal-phenomenology material generates predictions falsifiable against empirical literature on subjective time perception, the well-documented age-acceleration phenomenon, the time-distortion of flow and danger, and the structure of memory and anticipation. It also surfaces real open problems — particularly around dreams and altered states — that warrant focused future work.

The architecture's strength is its capacity to be applied. This is the first application paper. Subsequent applications — narrative self, differentiated affect, dreams and altered states, intersubjectivity beyond the prerogative of consistency, agency phenomenology, aesthetic experience, and the formal derivation of the structural prerogatives — will each test whether the foundation has the explanatory reach this application has demonstrated. The foundation is intended to be stable enough to support that work.
